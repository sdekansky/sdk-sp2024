"From Spaniard and Black, a Mullata is Born Depicts a Racial Stereotype of New Spain. This chaotic kitchen scene, painted by an unknown artist, features a white Spaniard fending off his black wife, who brandishes a wooden spoon as if to strike. Their mixed child intervenes as onlookers, both mixed and black, watch in surprise. The painting perpetuates the racist stereotype of black women as aggressive, amusing the white male gaze. As typical in casta paintings, the mixed child serves as a mediator of conflict, a common motif within the genre."

Felix Parra’s Chobala Massacre Portrays the Cruelty of Conquest. Completed in 1877, this painting depicts Hernán Cortez's attack on Chobala, resulting in the death of six thousand people. Cortez stands triumphantly amidst looted treasures, with a deceased indigenous man at his feet, resembling Christ's crucifixion. The wife holds their presumably deceased child, symbolizing the permanent alteration of Mexico's future. Three surviving natives sit amidst scattered possessions in despair.

Gabriel Guerra's Cuauhtemoc's Torture Depicts Conquistador Brutality for the Public. Created in 1886, this panel adorns the Monument of Cuauhtémoc in Mexico City, depicting the last Aztec emperor bound and tortured by Spanish conquistadors. His agony symbolizes the trauma inflicted upon indigenous peoples during colonization. Placed in a public space, the monument serves as a reminder of Mexico's indigenous heritage and resistance against colonial aggression.

José Clemente Orozco’s The Epic of American Civilization Critiques Mexico’s Complicated Past. Completed in 1932 for Dartmouth College, this mural is divided into two sections portraying conquest, conflict, and societal transformation. The first section, "The Coming and Parting of Quetzalcoatl," visualizes indigenous rituals and the arrival of the conquistadors. It contrasts human sacrifice with modern sacrifice for societal stability. The second section, "Cortez and Modern America," depicts Cortez's conquest and the evolution of Mexican society, critiquing oppressive systems using symbols of industrialization and societal transformation.
